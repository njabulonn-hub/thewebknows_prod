Network What the internet sees about your connection

Public IP (IPv4) Detecting your public IPv4 address so you can confirm what the web sees.
Learn More +

What is a public IPv4 Address?

IPv4 addresses are 32-bit numbers written as four decimals separated by dots (for example 192.0.2.1). The format supports about 4.3 billion unique connections and still powers most of the public internet.

How is your Public IP Address used legitimately?

Legitimate services rely on IPv4 for routing, meeting regional content rules, detecting fraud, and tailoring content to your location.

How could your Public IP Address be used maliciously?

Attackers combine IPv4 with other signals to profile you, launch targeted attacks, or approximate where you live or work.

Is this accurate?

This value is known because it is the exact IPv4 address your connection broadcasts to every site you visit.

Can I hide my Public IP Address?

Use a trustworthy VPN, proxy, or privacy network to mask your IPv4 address. Remember that VPN providers can still see your real address and some services block shared VPN ranges.

Public IP (IPv6) Detecting whether your connection advertises an IPv6 address alongside IPv4.
Learn More +

What is a public IPv6 Address?

IPv6 addresses are 128-bit numbers written as eight groups of hexadecimal characters separated by colons. The space holds roughly 340 undecillion addresses, removing the supply crunch seen with IPv4.

How is your Public IPv6 Address used legitimately?

IPv6 performs the same routing, compliance, and device-assignment duties as IPv4 while supporting the billions of devices connected to the modern internet.

How could your Public IPv6 Address be used maliciously?

Exposed IPv6 addresses can be combined with other telemetry for tracking or to target attacks at your network.

Is this accurate?

This is known when your ISP enables IPv6. If nothing appears, your provider is not broadcasting IPv6 to the web.

Can I hide my Public IPv6 Address?

Use a VPN that supports IPv6 or disable IPv6 on your device or router if you do not want the address exposed.

What are your ISP and ASN?

Your Internet Service Provider is the company delivering your connection, while the Autonomous System Number identifies the network that announces your traffic to the internet.

How are your ISP and ASN used legitimately?

How could your ISP and ASN be used maliciously?

Is this accurate?

This information is known and drawn from public routing databases that map IP ranges to ISPs.

Can I hide my ISP or ASN?

Connecting through a VPN or privacy relay will show the VPN operator instead of your residential ISP.

What Websites Can't See Information that remains private without your permission

Websites cannot see the following without your explicit permission:

  • Your computer username — Your device's user account name is private
  • Your email address — Unless you sign in or enter it, websites don't know your email
  • Your keyboard, mouse, speakers, or printer models — Browsers do not reveal the make or model of connected peripherals
  • Your storage type or drive health — Websites can't tell if you're using an SSD, HDD, or anything else
  • Your files or personal documents — Your device's files remain private

Frequently Asked Questions Common questions about website tracking and privacy

What is an IP address? +

An Internet Protocol (IP) address is a unique numerical label assigned to every device connected to a network. It's the fundamental mechanism that allows data to find its way to your computer, tablet, or phone from anywhere on the internet.

Think of your IP address as the digital equivalent of your home's postal address. Just as a letter needs a destination address to reach you, a website's server needs your IP address to send the data you requested.

This address reveals key information about your connection:

  • Your Approximate Location: Typically your city or region, not your precise street address.
  • Your Internet Service Provider (ISP): The company you pay for your internet access.
  • Your Network Type: For instance, whether it's a residential, mobile, or business connection.

Websites, apps, and advertising technology routinely log IP addresses. They use this data for analytics (measuring traffic), fraud prevention (spotting suspicious login attempts), and geolocation (showing you local news or prices in your currency).

Can a website owner see my browsing history? +

No. A website cannot see your full browsing history from other sites. The information it can typically see is limited to:

  • The pages you visit on its own site.
  • Technical information like your IP address, device type, browser version, and the "referrer" page (the site you came from).

Who can see more? Your Internet Service Provider (ISP), your employer/school network admin, or anyone with access to your device can see more of your overall browsing activity. Using a VPN or Tor Browser can help protect your traffic from these observers.

Can a website see my screen? +

Not by default. A standard website cannot see your screen, other open tabs, or applications.

The only exception is if you explicitly grant permission for screen sharing, such as during a video call on Zoom, Google Meet, or Microsoft Teams in your browser. If you never click "Allow," they cannot see your screen.

How do I know if a website is tracking me? +

Signs that you are being tracked include:

  • Third-party cookies from ad networks and analytics companies.
  • Retargeted ads that "follow" you across different websites.
  • Cookie consent banners that mention "personalized ads," "partners," or "marketing."
  • Your browser's privacy tools (like Brave Shields or Firefox's Enhanced Tracking Protection) showing blocked trackers.
Can I be tracked after visiting a website? +

Yes, tracking can persist after you leave a site through several methods:

  • Tracking Cookies: Small files stored on your device that remember your visit.
  • Browser Fingerprinting: Creating a unique profile based on your browser, fonts, screen size, and other device characteristics.
  • Logged-in Accounts: Services like Google or Facebook can track your activity across sites that use their features (like "Like" buttons or ads).

Using private browsing, tracker blockers, and logging out of accounts reduces tracking but does not make you completely anonymous.

Can a website detect if I take a screenshot? +

On normal web pages: No. Websites cannot detect if you use your operating system's screenshot shortcut (e.g., Print Screen or Cmd+Shift+4).

They can sometimes detect actions within the browser, such as:

  • Right-clicking and selecting "Save image as..."
  • Copying text from the page.
How can I disable or block website tracking? +

Basic Steps:

  • In your browser settings, enable "Block third-party cookies" and turn on "Enhanced Tracking Protection" or its equivalent.
  • Use a privacy-focused search engine like DuckDuckGo or Startpage.
  • Install a trusted content-blocking extension like uBlock Origin.

Stronger Protection:

  • Use a privacy-focused browser like Brave, Firefox (in Strict mode), or the DuckDuckGo browser.
  • Use a VPN to hide your browsing data from your ISP.
  • For maximum anonymity, use the Tor Browser.

Note: Some websites may not work correctly with heavy tracking protection enabled.

How can I detect if I'm being tracked? +
  • Check your browser's built-in Privacy Report or "Site Information" panel (often indicated by a shield icon in the address bar) to see blocked trackers and cookies.
  • Notice if you see ads for products you recently searched for on other sites.
  • Specialist browser extensions can show you all the tracking scripts loading on a page in real-time.
What cookies should I not accept? +

You should be most cautious about accepting:

  • Third-Party / Advertising Cookies
  • Cookies categorized as being for "Personalization," "Marketing," or "Data Sharing"

It is generally safe (and often necessary for the site to function) to accept:

  • Strictly Necessary / Essential Cookies (e.g., for logins, shopping carts, and security).

When possible, always choose "Reject All" for non-essential cookies.

Which browser does not track you? +

No browser is 100% tracking-free, but some are designed with privacy as a default priority:

  • Brave: Blocks ads, trackers, and fingerprinting by default.
  • Tor Browser: Routes your traffic through the Tor network for strong anonymity, though it can be slower.
  • Firefox (with Strict Tracking Protection): A highly configurable browser that blocks many trackers and is improving its anti-fingerprinting features.
  • DuckDuckGo Browser: Available on mobile and desktop, it automatically blocks trackers.

Most browsers, including these, may collect some anonymized usage data, which can usually be disabled in the settings.

Which is the world's safest browser? +

There is no single "safest" browser, as it depends on your needs:

  • For Maximum Anonymity: Tor Browser is widely considered the best due to its multi-layered encryption and strict anti-fingerprinting measures.
  • For Everyday Privacy (Balancing Safety and Usability): Brave, Firefox, and DuckDuckGo consistently rank highly in security and privacy reviews.

Remember, "safety" also depends on your habits: keeping your browser updated, using strong passwords, and being cautious about the extensions you install.

How to Find Your IP Address on Windows +

Every device on a network has an IP address. Think of it like your computer's "apartment number" on the internet or in your home.

  • Your Public IP is the address your entire home or office network uses to talk to the internet. It's shared by all your devices. Websites see this address when you visit them.
  • Your Local IP is the unique address for your specific computer inside your home or office network. Your router uses this to send data to the right device.

Finding Your PUBLIC IP Address (How the internet sees you)

Easy Method (Using a Website):

This is the simplest way for most people.

  1. Open any web browser (like Microsoft Edge, Google Chrome, or Firefox).
  2. Go to this website: thewebknows.com
  3. The first thing you'll see on the page is your Public IP Address. It will also show your internet provider and a general location.

Advanced Method (Using Command Prompt - No Browser):

If you can't use a browser, you can use a text-based tool.

  1. Click the Start menu (the Windows icon), type cmd, and press Enter. This opens the Command Prompt—a black window with text.
  2. Carefully type (or copy and paste) the following command and press Enter: 
    nslookup myip.opendns.com resolver1.opendns.com
  3. Look for the line that says "Non-authoritative answer:". Right below that, you will see your Public IP address listed next to the second "Address:" line.

Finding Your LOCAL IP Address (Your computer's internal address)

Simple Method (Using Settings):

  1. Click the Start menu → then click the Settings gear icon (or type "Settings").
  2. Go to Network & Internet.

If you are on Wi-Fi:

  1. Click Wi-Fi.
  2. Click the name of the Wi-Fi network you are connected to.
  3. Scroll down, and you will see your "IPv4 address"—this is your local IP.

If you are using an Ethernet cable:

  1. Click Ethernet.
  2. Click the name of your network connection.
  3. Your "IPv4 address" will be listed there.

Alternate Method (Using Command Prompt):

  1. Open Command Prompt as described above.
  2. Simply type ipconfig and press Enter.
  3. A list of information will appear. Look for either:
    • "Wireless LAN adapter Wi-Fi" (if you're on Wi-Fi), or
    • "Ethernet adapter Ethernet" (if you're using a cable).
  4. Find the line that says "IPv4 Address". The number next to it is your local IP address.
How to Find Your IP Address on a Mac +

Just like on Windows, your Mac has both a public and a local IP address.

Finding Your PUBLIC IP Address (How the internet sees you)

Easy Method (Using a Website):

  1. Open Safari or any other web browser.
  2. Go to: thewebknows.com
  3. Your Public IP Address will be displayed at the top of the page.

Advanced Method (Using Terminal - No Browser):

You can use the Mac's built-in Terminal app.

  1. Press Command (⌘) + Spacebar to open Spotlight Search, type "Terminal", and press Enter.
  2. Copy and paste this command and press Enter: 
    dig +short myip.opendns.com @resolver1.opendns.com
  3. The only thing that should appear is your Public IP address.

Finding Your LOCAL IP Address (Your Mac's internal address)

Simple Method (Using System Settings):

  1. Click the Apple menu () in the top-left corner and select System Settings.
  2. Go to Network.
  3. Select your active connection (it will say "Connected" next to Wi-Fi or your Ethernet service).
  4. Click Details... next to the network name.
  5. Click on the TCP/IP tab. Your local IP address is listed next to "IPv4 Address".

Alternate Method (Using Terminal):

  1. Open Terminal as described above.
  2. To find the address for your Wi-Fi, type this command and press Enter: 
    ipconfig getifaddr en0
    To find the address for your Ethernet connection, use this command instead: 
    ipconfig getifaddr en1
  3. The number that appears is your local IP address.
How do I find my router IP address? +

Your router's IP address is also called the "default gateway" address. Here's how to find it:

On Windows:

  • Press Start, type cmd, and press Enter.
  • Type ipconfig and press Enter.
  • Look for Default Gateway under your active network adapter (Wi-Fi or Ethernet). This is your router's IP address (typically something like 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1).

On macOS:

  • Open Terminal (press ⌘ + Space, type Terminal).
  • Type netstat -nr | grep default and press Enter.
  • The first IP address shown is your router's IP address.
  • Alternatively, go to System Settings → Network → select your connection → Details... → TCP/IP. The Router field shows your router's IP.

Common Router IP Addresses: Most routers use 192.168.1.1, 192.168.0.1, or 10.0.0.1. You can also check the label on your router or its manual.
