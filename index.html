What Websites Already Know About You

We've scanned your device and compiled what websites can readily see about you and your device.

Use the "Learn More" links to understand each data point, how it's typically used and practical implications.

Network What the internet sees about your connection

Public IP (IPv4) Detecting your public IPv4 address so you can confirm what the web sees.
Learn More +

What is a public IPv4 Address?

IPv4 addresses are 32-bit numbers written as four decimals separated by dots (for example 192.0.2.1). The format supports about 4.3 billion unique connections and still powers most of the public internet.

How is your Public IP Address used legitimately?

Legitimate services rely on IPv4 for routing, meeting regional content rules, detecting fraud, and tailoring content to your location.

How could your Public IP Address be used maliciously?

Attackers combine IPv4 with other signals to profile you, launch targeted attacks, or approximate where you live or work.

Is this accurate?

This value is known because it is the exact IPv4 address your connection broadcasts to every site you visit.

Can I hide my Public IP Address?

Use a trustworthy VPN, proxy, or privacy network to mask your IPv4 address. Remember that VPN providers can still see your real address and some services block shared VPN ranges.

Public IP (IPv6) Detecting whether your connection advertises an IPv6 address alongside IPv4.
Learn More +

What is a public IPv6 Address?

IPv6 addresses are 128-bit numbers written as eight groups of hexadecimal characters separated by colons. The space holds roughly 340 undecillion addresses, removing the supply crunch seen with IPv4.

How is your Public IPv6 Address used legitimately?

IPv6 performs the same routing, compliance, and device-assignment duties as IPv4 while supporting the billions of devices connected to the modern internet.

How could your Public IPv6 Address be used maliciously?

Exposed IPv6 addresses can be combined with other telemetry for tracking or to target attacks at your network.

Is this accurate?

This is known when your ISP enables IPv6. If nothing appears, your provider is not broadcasting IPv6 to the web.

Can I hide my Public IPv6 Address?

Use a VPN that supports IPv6 or disable IPv6 on your device or router if you do not want the address exposed.

ISP / ASN Identifying the ISP and autonomous system number linked to your IP address.
Learn More +

What are your ISP and ASN?

Your Internet Service Provider is the company delivering your connection, while the Autonomous System Number identifies the network that announces your traffic to the internet.

How are your ISP and ASN used legitimately?

Websites use this data to understand connection quality, apply local licensing rules, detect VPNs, and tailor experiences to home or business networks.

How could your ISP and ASN be used maliciously?

Attackers can cross-reference ISP data to guess your location, profile customers of a provider, or target vulnerabilities affecting that network.

Is this accurate?

This information is known and drawn from public routing databases that map IP ranges to ISPs.

Can I hide my ISP or ASN?

Connecting through a VPN or privacy relay will show the VPN operator instead of your residential ISP.

Location (IP-based) Estimating your city and region from public IP geolocation databases.
Learn More +

What is your IP-based location?

This is an IP-based guess at your location that is typically accurate to the city or metro area but not to a precise street address.

How is your IP-based location used legitimately?

Websites use location to localize pricing, language, compliance messages, and fraud controls.

How could your IP-based location be used maliciously?

Bad actors can combine this with other data points to stalk targets, launch region-specific scams, or infer daily habits.

Is this accurate?

This is inferred from commercial geolocation databases and can be wrong, especially when you use VPNs or corporate routes.

Can I hide my IP-based location?

A VPN or privacy relay can make it appear that you are in a different city; keep in mind this may impact access to localized services.

Referrer Capturing the referring page or noting when privacy settings strip the referrer header.
Learn More +

What is your referrer?

The referrer reveals which site or search engine sent you here; it is automatically shared when you click most links.

How is your referrer used legitimately?

Websites analyze referrers to understand acquisition channels, pay affiliates, and fix navigation issues.

How could your referrer be used maliciously?

Referrers can expose sensitive query strings or reveal your browsing habits when tracked across multiple sites.

Is this accurate?

Referrer values are known because browsers send them automatically unless a privacy control removes the header.

Can I hide my referrer?

Most modern browsers and privacy extensions can strip referrers; note that doing so may affect affiliate attribution or break some embedded content.

Network type (guess)Guessing whether your IP looks residential, mobile, or like a VPN exit.
Learn More +

What does your network type guess mean?

We compare your ISP name with known residential, mobile, and hosting providers to infer whether your traffic resembles a home line or anonymized relay.

How is your network type guess used legitimately?

Legitimate businesses use this signal to trigger extra anti-fraud checks, throttle automated bots, or adapt experiences for corporate networks.

How could your network type guess be used maliciously?

Malicious actors may flag VPN usage, craft more believable phishing lures, or bypass security settings aimed specifically at residential users.

Is this accurate?

This is an educated guess based on naming patterns and may mislabel niche or newly announced networks.

Can I change my network type guess?

Because the label is inferred, the only way to change it is to switch providers or use a VPN that mimics a residential connection.

Reverse DNSChecking the hostname attached to your IP address, when one exists.
Learn More +

What is your reverse DNS hostname?

Reverse DNS looks up a readable hostname from your IP, similar to looking up a phone number to learn the caller name.

How is your reverse DNS hostname used legitimately?

Network teams rely on reverse DNS for email authentication, traffic routing, and debugging connectivity issues.

How could your reverse DNS hostname be used maliciously?

Attackers can inspect the hostname to learn which ISP you use or whether other devices share the same network.

Is this accurate?

It is known only if your ISP publishes a reverse DNS record; many residential IPs do not have one.

Can I hide my reverse DNS hostname?

You cannot edit reverse DNS directly, but using a VPN will surface the VPN provider’s hostname instead.

What Websites Can't See Information that remains private without your permission

Websites cannot see the following without your explicit permission:

  • Your computer username — Your device's user account name is private
  • Your email address — Unless you sign in or enter it, websites don't know your email
  • Your keyboard, mouse, speakers, or printer models — Browsers do not reveal the make or model of connected peripherals
  • Your storage type or drive health — Websites can't tell if you're using an SSD, HDD, or anything else
  • Your files or personal documents — Your device's files remain private

This site never asks for any permissions. Everything shown here is available to any website without asking.
