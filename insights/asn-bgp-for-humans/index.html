ASN & BGP for Humans: Understand Your Network’s Passport

11 November 2025 14 min read

Overview: Autonomous System Numbers (ASNs) and the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) form the invisible scaffolding that keeps the internet stitched together. Every time you browse the web, your traffic advertises the ASN of the network that carried it. That single number hands receiving services a wealth of context: whether you are on a residential ISP, a mobile carrier, a multinational corporation, or a public cloud. This article demystifies ASNs and BGP for non-specialists, shows how they influence privacy, and walks you through the tools experts use to interpret the global routing map.

Overview (TL;DR)

An Autonomous System (AS) is a network—or collection of networks—run by one organisation under a single routing policy. Every AS has a unique ASN, such as AS15169 for Google or AS5089 for Virgin Media. BGP is the routing protocol that lets ASes exchange reachability information, deciding how packets traverse the internet. When a website sees your IP, it can instantly look up the associated ASN, revealing your provider type and often your country. ASNs underpin fraud detection, content licensing, and performance engineering. You cannot mask your ASN without changing networks, but you can understand what yours says about you and use VPNs or Tor when you need to present a different network identity.

What Is an Autonomous System?

Think of an AS as a self-governing island on the internet. Each AS controls a set of IP address ranges (prefixes) and decides how traffic enters and leaves its domain. The motivations vary:

  • Consumer ISPs (e.g., BT, Comcast, Vodafone) operate large ASes to connect homes and businesses.
  • Mobile carriers run separate ASes optimised for cellular backhaul.
  • Cloud providers like AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud operate global ASes spanning data centres.
  • Enterprises and universities sometimes maintain their own ASes to control routing policies and connect directly to multiple upstream providers.

The numeric ASN is assigned by a Regional Internet Registry (RIR). In Europe, RIPE NCC allocates ASNs; in North America, ARIN does. There are two formats: 16-bit “short” ASNs (0–65535) and 32-bit “long” ASNs (introduced as the internet expanded). Both work identically.

BGP: The Internet’s Routing Glue

BGP is often called the internet’s postman. It is a path-vector protocol: ASes advertise the IP prefixes they can reach, along with the AS-path—the sequence of ASNs a packet must traverse to get there. Routers select the “best” path based on policies that weigh path length, commercial relationships, and performance metrics.

Consider a user on AS5089 (Virgin Media) opening a site hosted on AS13335 (Cloudflare). Virgin’s routers receive BGP announcements from their upstream providers indicating how to reach Cloudflare. They choose the preferred route, append their own ASN to the AS-path, and forward traffic accordingly. Cloudflare sees the incoming connection and notes the AS-path, learning it traversed AS5089 via AS3356 (Lumen) before reaching them.

BGP’s flexibility enables complex commercial arrangements—transit, settlement-free peering, hybrid peering—but it was built on trust. Malicious or misconfigured ASes can announce prefixes they do not own (BGP hijacking), redirecting traffic. Initiatives like Route Origin Authorisation (ROA) and Resource Public Key Infrastructure (RPKI) aim to secure the system, but adoption remains uneven.

Why Services Care About Your ASN

Because an ASN represents a bounded, identifiable network, it instantly tells websites what “kind” of user they are dealing with. Common interpretations include:

  • Residential vs. data centre: Traffic from AS7922 (Comcast Cable) is treated differently to traffic from AS16509 (Amazon). The former implies a household, the latter a server or VPN.
  • Mobile vs. fixed-line: Mobile ASNs (e.g., AS21928 for T-Mobile US) come with higher churn, higher latency, and shared IP pools, influencing fraud scores.
  • Corporate attribution: A request from AS39757 might identify Barclays Bank; sales teams use this to tailor outreach.
  • Jurisdiction and licensing: Broadcasters use ASNs to enforce region-specific content rights. Some countries block or throttle specific ASNs to control access.
  • Security heuristics: Attackers frequently operate from cloud ASNs. Defensive systems build allow-lists for trusted enterprise ASNs while scrutinising others.

In short, ASNs are high-level identity tokens that require no cookies, no JavaScript, and no consent banners.

Peering, Transit, and the Economics of Routing

Understanding the business relationships between ASes explains why your traffic might take seemingly odd routes:

  • Transit is a customer-supplier relationship. A smaller AS pays a larger one for full internet access. Traffic can traverse multiple tiers: local ISP → national carrier → Tier 1 provider.
  • Peering is settlement-free exchange. Two ASes of comparable size agree to swap traffic directly, usually for mutual benefit and lower latency. This often occurs at Internet Exchange Points (IXPs) such as LINX (London) or DE-CIX (Frankfurt).
  • Partial transit and paid peering occupy the middle ground, blending financial arrangements with performance goals.

Peering policies are public: many networks publish “peeringDB” entries describing where and how they connect. For privacy-aware users, understanding your ISP’s peering footprint explains why certain services feel snappier or why latency spikes when a peering dispute erupts.

Anycast and BGP Engineering Tricks

Modern internet infrastructure exploits BGP to achieve redundancy and performance:

  • Anycast: Operators announce the same IP prefix from multiple locations. BGP steers users to the “closest” site based on routing policy. Cloudflare’s DNS, Google’s 8.8.8.8, and many DDoS mitigation services use anycast to absorb attacks and deliver low latency.
  • Traffic engineering: ASes manipulate BGP attributes—prepends, MEDs, communities—to nudge traffic along preferred paths, balancing load or avoiding congested links.
  • Geosharding: Streaming platforms announce different prefixes per region, ensuring users stay within licensing boundaries even if they share the same service brand.

From a privacy standpoint, these techniques mean the IP- to-location relationship can change based on network conditions. Even if geolocation says “Amsterdam”, anycast might route you to a London cache because of congestion—a reminder that IP intelligence is probabilistic.

Inspecting ASNs Yourself

Several tools make it easy to interrogate ASNs without specialist hardware:

Web toolkits

  • bgp.he.net (Hurricane Electric) lets you look up any ASN or IP, view announced prefixes, peers, and historical changes.
  • Qrator Radar monitors BGP incidents, highlighting hijacks and leaks.
  • RIPEstat offers visualisations, geolocation, and routing consistency checks.

Command-line favourites

  • whois <IP> reveals the IP’s organisation and ASN (via the origin field).
  • traceroute or mtr (My Traceroute) show each hop; router hostnames often encode ASNs or city codes.
  • bgpq3 fetches prefix lists for a given ASN, useful when constructing firewall policies that allow or deny specific networks.

Armed with these tools, you can audit where your ISP peers, confirm whether a VPN uses reputable networks, and detect when traffic suddenly shifts—often a sign of BGP misconfiguration or an outage.

Privacy Implications and Mitigations

Because ASNs are network-level identifiers, changing them requires changing the network path. Your options include:

  • VPNs: Connecting to a VPN causes websites to see the VPN provider’s ASN instead of your home ISP’s. Premium services operate their own ASNs; others rent servers in third-party ASNs, which is why some VPN IPs are easy to flag.
  • Tor: Tor exit nodes sit in a variety of ASNs, often in data centres. The exit’s ASN will be visible to destinations. While this masks your origin, exits themselves can be blocked by services wary of abuse.
  • Corporate split tunnelling: Some enterprises deliberately expose staff traffic via company ASNs for branding or security. If you want personal browsing to appear residential, avoid corporate VPNs for personal tasks.

Ultimately, understanding your ASN is about context. Recognise that every outbound connection announces it, and plan accordingly when anonymity, commercial secrecy, or geo-sensitive access matters.

Security: BGP Hijacking and RPKI

BGP’s trust-based design occasionally fails spectacularly. When an AS accidentally (or maliciously) announces someone else’s prefix, traffic is misrouted. Examples include:

  • 2008 Pakistan vs. YouTube: Pakistan Telecom attempted to block YouTube domestically, but the BGP announcement leaked globally, blackholing the platform for hours.
  • 2018 Route leaks via Nigeria: A small ISP accidentally redirected traffic for Google, Cloudflare, and Amazon through a Russian ISP, causing widespread disruption.

To combat this, the industry is deploying RPKI. Network operators cryptographically sign which AS is authorised to originate each prefix. Routers that validate RPKI can reject invalid announcements. When choosing providers—VPNs, hosting companies, upstream ISPs—prefer those that implement RPKI; it signals modern operational practices.

Corporate and Compliance Use Cases

ASNs influence more than routing:

  • Due diligence: Security teams monitor outbound connections for unexpected ASNs, flagging shadow IT or malware using unusual tunnels.
  • Regulatory reporting: Some jurisdictions require data residency. Organisations map ASNs to prove that traffic stays within approved borders.
  • Incident response: During attacks, knowing which AS a hostile IP belongs to speeds up escalation to the right abuse contacts.

For privacy teams, documenting the ASNs involved in data flows supports risk assessments and vendor evaluations.

FAQ

Can I hide or change my ASN without a VPN?

No. Your ASN is determined by the network you connect through. To present a different ASN, you must route via another network (VPN, Tor, proxy, mobile hotspot, etc.).

Is an ASN the same as an ISP?

Not exactly, but they are closely related. An ISP may operate multiple ASNs for different services or regions. Conversely, some ASNs belong to enterprises that are not ISPs. The ASN is the technical identifier; the ISP is the business entity.

Does BGP reveal my exact location?

No. BGP reveals the AS-path, not your address. However, ASNs are often geographically scoped, and router hostnames may include city codes. Combined with IP geolocation, they narrow down your probable region.

Is BGP secure?

Historically, no. BGP trusts its neighbours. Efforts like RPKI and BGP monitoring platforms improve security, but misconfigurations still happen. Be prepared for occasional anomalies.

Key Takeaway

Your ASN is the network-level identity card you present to the world. It informs how websites trust you, how regulators categorise you, and how efficiently your traffic flows. You cannot opt out, but you can understand the implications, monitor how your ASN is perceived, and switch networks when you need to adopt a different posture. Mastering ASN and BGP basics turns the internet from a mysterious cloud into a map you can read—and privacy is far easier to manage when you know the terrain.
