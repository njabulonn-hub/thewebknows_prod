Cookies, Storage APIs & Service Workers: Tracking in 2025

11 November 2025 13 min read

Overview: Cookies have not vanished, but they are no longer the sole identifier in town. Modern trackers blend first-party cookies, storage APIs, service workers, and clever partition-busting tactics to build resilient user profiles. The good news is that browsers have responded with SameSite defaults, storage partitioning, and stricter service-worker lifecycles. This article maps the evolving stack and sets out pragmatic defences that preserve site functionality.

Overview (TL;DR)

Tracking in 2025 is layered. Functional first-party cookies remain indispensable, while third-party cookies are being phased out or throttled. Trackers compensate by leaning on localStorage, sessionStorage, IndexedDB, and Cache API—often orchestrated by long-lived service workers. Browser vendors counter these tactics with partitioned storage, token budgets, and stricter permissions. Users can stay in control by embracing privacy-centric browsers, reviewing stored data regularly, compartmentalising browsing contexts, and pruning unnecessary service workers or extensions.

The Cookie Jar: First Party, Third Party, and SameSite Defaults

Cookies are tiny key–value pairs set by HTTP response headers or JavaScript. Their power comes from being automatically sent with future requests to the same domain, allowing servers to recognise returning browsers. Understanding the types matters:

  • First-party cookies originate from the domain in your address bar. They underpin session management, shopping baskets, and regional preferences. Removing them can log you out or reset configuration.
  • Third-party cookies are set by embedded domains—advertising networks, analytics providers, single sign-on vendors. They enable cross-site recognition and, historically, behavioural advertising profiles. Safari, Firefox, and Brave now block or partition them by default; Chrome is phasing them out via the Privacy Sandbox.

The SameSite attribute determines when cookies travel with requests:

  • SameSite=Strict cookies stay within the first-party context. They protect against cross-site request forgery but can break flows that rely on sharing state across tabs.
  • SameSite=Lax allows cookies on top-level navigation (e.g., clicking a link) but not on cross-site subresources. This is Chrome’s default, striking a balance between security and usability.
  • SameSite=None demands Secure (HTTPS) and keeps cookies available in all contexts. It is reserved for legacy integrations that truly need third-party behaviour.

Understanding SameSite is crucial because it dictates whether your defensive strategy should rely on browser settings, server-side mitigations, or extension-based blocking.

Beyond Cookies: Storage APIs in the Spotlight

Restricting third-party cookies nudged trackers to explore other stateful surfaces. The main contenders are native browser storage APIs, each with unique properties:

localStorage and sessionStorage

These simple key–value stores are scoped to the origin (protocol + host + port). localStorage persists indefinitely until cleared, while sessionStorage dies when the tab closes. They are not automatically sent to servers yet can be read by any script running within the page, including injected third-party code. Malicious actors store unique identifiers here, synchronising them with servers through regular AJAX calls. Because many users never clear storage, identifiers can survive long after cookies perish.

IndexedDB

IndexedDB is a full-blown NoSQL-style database in the browser, capable of storing megabytes of structured information. Progressive web apps use it to cache emails, documents, or product catalogues for offline access. Trackers can exploit this headroom to persist rich profiles, encryption keys, or “evercookies” composed of redundant data chunks that repopulate other storage areas if deleted.

Cache API and the Fetch Event

The Cache API—available to service workers—stores entire request/response pairs. Its legitimate purpose is offline functionality and performance. However, it also offers a covert channel: a worker can cache a fake image whose filename encodes a user identifier, retrieve it later, and rebuild a history across subdomains. Without auditing the cache, users rarely notice this persistence layer.

In short, stripping cookies is not enough. You must treat every local storage surface as a potential tracking vector.

Storage Partitioning: The Game-Changer

To counter cross-site tracking via storage, browsers introduced storage partitioning, also known as double-keying. Instead of scoping storage solely by origin, browsers scope it by (top-level site, embedded origin). A third-party script loaded on two different websites receives two isolated storage buckets. The identifier it sets while embedded on news.example is invisible when it appears on shop.example.

Safari pioneered Intelligent Tracking Prevention, expiring third-party storage quickly and partitioning it per first party. Firefox adopted Total Cookie Protection, applying the same concept across cookies, localStorage, IndexedDB, and more. Chrome is rolling out CHIPS (Cookies Having Independent Partitioned State) and similar mechanisms for other storage surfaces as part of Privacy Sandbox.

Partitioning does not make trackers powerless; they can still build detailed first-party dossiers. Yet it dramatically reduces passive cross-site correlation, pushing advertisers towards cohort-based measurement and first-party data partnerships.

Service Workers: The Persistent Middlemen

Service workers run in the background, intercepting network requests, delivering push notifications, and enabling offline experiences. Their scope is limited to the origin that registered them, but within that origin they are potent tools for persistence.

  • Longevity: Once installed, a worker can wake via a push event or background sync, even when no tab is open. Malicious scripts can use this to replant identifiers after a user clears cookies or storage.
  • Network interception: Workers can capture fetch events, modify responses, or insert headers. A privacy-invasive site could silently beacon browsing history or inject marketers’ scripts into every page view.
  • Bootstrapping tracking: A worker can populate localStorage, IndexedDB, and caches with redundant identifiers, creating “evercookie” resilience across APIs.

Modern browsers mitigate abuse via tightened lifecycles. Chrome introduces budget-based background updates, limiting how often dormant workers can awaken without user interaction. Firefox requires secure origins and warns when multiple workers overconsume resources. Still, auditing service workers remains an essential privacy hygiene step.

How Trackers Combine These Layers

Modern tracking stacks increasingly resemble fault-tolerant systems. A typical workflow might look like this:

  1. On first visit, the site sets a first-party cookie with a unique identifier and mirrors it into localStorage and IndexedDB.
  2. A service worker registers silently, caching a crafted response whose filename matches the identifier. It also schedules a background sync to report engagement metrics.
  3. If the cookie is later deleted, the worker repopulates it from IndexedDB. If IndexedDB is wiped, the worker reconstructs the identifier from the cached response name.
  4. When embedded as a third-party script on partner sites, storage partitioning blocks direct access, so the tracker relies on redirect tricks (bounce tracking) to shift the user into a first-party context briefly, refreshing the master identifier.

The system is resilient because it does not rely on a single storage type. Defending against it requires layered countermeasures rather than a one-off tweak.

Practical Defences Without Breaking Apps

Privacy hygiene should balance protection with usability. Overly aggressive blocking can lock you out of banking, collaboration suites, or government portals. Consider this layered approach:

1. Pick a Browser with Opinionated Defaults

Firefox with Enhanced Tracking Protection and Total Cookie Protection offers a mature balance between privacy and compatibility. Brave goes further by stripping or randomising fingerprinting surfaces by default. Safari enforces strict partitioning, though some web apps still expect legacy behaviour.

2. Embrace Cookie and Site-Data Hygiene

  • Enable settings that auto-delete cookies and site data when the browser closes, but add exceptions for essential services such as banking.
  • Schedule a monthly audit: in Chrome/Edge visit Settings → Privacy and security → Cookies and other site data → See all site data. In Firefox go to Settings → Privacy & Security → Cookies and Site Data → Manage Data. Remove stale entries and anything you do not recognise.

3. Partition Your Browsing Contexts

Firefox Multi-Account Containers, Chrome profiles, and Safari profiles allow you to segregate work, personal, and research browsing. Each profile maintains separate cookies, storage, and service workers, limiting cross-contamination.

4. Monitor and Curb Service Workers

  • In Chrome/Edge visit chrome://serviceworker-internals/ to list active workers. Unregister anything you do not recognise.
  • In Firefox open about:debugging#/runtime/this-firefox and remove stale workers. Consider disabling background sync for untrusted sites.
  • iOS and Android browsers expose similar debugging views within developer settings; make periodic checks part of your routine.

5. Employ Trusted Extensions Sparingly

Extensions such as uBlock Origin, ClearURLs, and Cookie AutoDelete add strong guardrails, but each extension increases your fingerprint uniqueness. Install only what you need, keep them updated, and prefer open-source projects with active maintenance.

6. Leverage Built-In Privacy Modes

Many browsers now offer profile-specific privacy toggles, such as Brave’s aggressive fingerprinting protection or Firefox’s Resist Fingerprinting flag. Enable them for sensitive research sessions, knowing that some sites may degrade gracefully to simpler layouts.

Enterprise and Developer Responsibilities

Developers and organisations have a duty to wield these technologies responsibly. A few guiding principles:

  • Purpose limitation: If you rely on service workers for push notifications, do not piggyback tracking identifiers. Keep functionality-focused storage separate from analytics stores.
  • Respect user choice: Honour Global Privacy Control (GPC) and consent banners by downgrading to essential-only storage when users opt out.
  • Implement data retention policies: Expire identifiers when they are no longer necessary. A “delete” button that only clears cookies but leaves IndexedDB intact undermines trust and may breach data-protection laws.
  • Document your stack: Privacy impact assessments should enumerate every storage API and worker used, the data stored, and the retention period. Regulators increasingly expect this granularity.

Regulatory Landscape and Compliance Notes

The UK GDPR and EU GDPR treat persistent identifiers as personal data when they can be linked to an individual. Consent must be informed and specific, meaning you cannot bury the mention of service-worker storage in a generic cookie banner. Supervisory authorities across Europe have issued fines for misconfigured consent flows, particularly where analytics cookies persisted despite a user rejecting tracking. The ePrivacy Directive (and its UK equivalent) also requires opt-in consent before storing or accessing information on a user’s device, regardless of the API used.

In practice, that means organisations need equal transparency for cookies, localStorage, IndexedDB, and service workers. Users who withdraw consent should trigger a full wipe across these surfaces. Automating this via clear API wrappers is safer than attempting manual clean-up per surface.

How to Audit Yourself

Run this diagnostic routine every quarter:

  1. Inspect cookies: Use your browser’s developer tools (Application tab in Chrome, Storage Inspector in Firefox) to list cookies. Note which domains set third-party cookies and whether their lifetimes seem excessive.
  2. Review localStorage/sessionStorage: Look for opaque identifiers or base64-encoded blobs. Search for keywords like id, token, or profile.
  3. Check IndexedDB: Identify databases created by unfamiliar origins. Some tracking scripts label their stores with vendor names; others hide behind GUIDs.
  4. Audit service workers: Remove any with suspicious scopes, such as workers registered by advertising domains rather than the site you intended to visit.
  5. Clear caches strategically: Delete entries within the Cache API that resemble tracking artefacts—tiny files with unique filenames, pixel images, or beacon responses. Keep legitimate offline caches for apps you trust.

This process sounds technical, but after one or two runs you will recognise patterns quickly.

Key Takeaway

The modern tracking stack is resilient by design, blending multiple storage primitives and background scripts. Your best defence is to embrace browsers that partition state, practise regular storage hygiene, and retain control over which sites earn long-term trust. Balanced privacy is achievable without returning to a brittle web experience.

FAQ

Do I need to delete cookies if I already block third-party cookies?

Yes. First-party cookies still reveal a lot about your behaviour on each site, and many trackers operate “in first-party guise” via hosted subdomains. Periodic deletion helps, especially if you do not rely on saved sessions.

Is localStorage more private than cookies?

Not inherently. While localStorage is not sent automatically with every request, any script loaded on the page can read it, including malicious third parties. Without partitioning, it can persist tracking identifiers just as effectively as cookies.

Can I block service workers entirely?

You can disable them in some browsers or via extensions, but doing so breaks progressive web apps, push notifications, and some offline-capable tools. A more sustainable approach is to audit workers, prune untrusted ones, and rely on browsers that curb abusive behaviour.

How often should I clear site data?

Monthly is a sensible baseline. Heavy web-app users might schedule weekly clear-downs for non-essential sites while preserving storage for tools that require offline caches or persistent logins.
