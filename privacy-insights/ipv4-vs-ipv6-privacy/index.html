IPv4 vs IPv6: Which Is Better for Privacy?

11 November 2025 13 min read

Overview: IPv6 was designed to solve IPv4 address exhaustion, but the shift from shared, NATed IPv4 addresses to abundant, globally routable IPv6 raises fresh privacy questions. Does giving every device its own public address make you easier to track? Do VPNs and firewalls still work the same way? This guide answers those questions, comparing IPv4 and IPv6 side by side, highlighting the privacy safeguards built into modern operating systems, and setting out the configuration checks that keep IPv6 from becoming an accidental leak.

Overview (TL;DR)

IPv4’s scarcity forced widespread Network Address Translation (NAT), which meant many devices shared a single public IP. That delivered a side-effect privacy benefit: outsiders could not easily distinguish between devices behind the same router. IPv6 eliminates the need for NAT by giving every interface a unique address, raising concerns about traceability. Fortunately, IPv6 Privacy Extensions (RFC 4941) randomise the dynamic part of the address for outbound connections, recreating the “shared identity” effect. With privacy extensions enabled—and with a VPN that tunnels IPv6—the privacy risk of IPv6 is comparable to IPv4. Major pitfalls include VPNs that ignore IPv6, routers that expose static interface IDs, and corporate networks that delegate too-large prefixes without controls.

Addressing Models: Scarcity vs. Abundance

The core difference between IPv4 and IPv6 lies in address space and how devices obtain addresses:

  • IPv4 uses 32-bit addresses (4.3 billion total). Residential ISPs typically allocate one public IPv4 to each router, with NAT mapping internal private addresses (192.168.0.0/16, 10.0.0.0/8) to that single public IP.
  • IPv6 adopts 128-bit addresses (340 undecillion possibilities). ISPs usually delegate a /56 or /64 prefix to each customer, giving every device multiple globally routable addresses.

This architectural shift means IPv6 restores end-to-end connectivity: no more NAT table entries, no more port forwarding quirks. From a privacy standpoint, however, it means a remote service could—at least in theory—tie every request to a single device because the address space is so large that sharing is unnecessary.

Understanding IPv6 Address Structure

An IPv6 address is typically split into two halves:

  • The network prefix (first 64 bits) identifies your ISP and, within that, the segment assigned to your connection.
  • The interface identifier (last 64 bits) identifies your device’s network interface.

Early IPv6 implementations derived the interface identifier from the device’s MAC address via the EUI-64 algorithm. That produced a stable identifier that persisted across networks—a privacy disaster. Privacy Extensions replaced this approach, generating pseudo-random interface IDs that change periodically. Some networks also deploy DHCPv6 to hand out addresses, allowing admin control over stability and rotation.

Privacy Extensions: The Hidden Hero

Privacy Extensions (PE) are the key reason IPv6 can be as private as IPv4 for day-to-day browsing. Enabled by default on Windows, macOS, iOS, Android, and modern Linux distributions, PE ensures that:

  • Each interface maintains a stable, non-routed address for inbound services (e.g., printers, file shares) and network management.
  • The operating system simultaneously generates a set of temporary, random addresses for outbound connections. These rotate every few hours and are discarded when no longer needed.
  • Applications prefer the temporary addresses when initiating outbound sessions. Servers only see the randomised address, making cross-session correlation difficult.

The result? Websites and trackers cannot rely on a static IPv6 address to follow you any more than they can rely on a dynamic IPv4 address behind NAT—provided privacy extensions remain enabled.

IPv4 NAT vs. IPv6 Firewalls

Another common misconception is that NAT equals security. In reality, the security came from the stateful firewall behaviour baked into most NAT routers: inbound connections were blocked unless explicitly permitted.

IPv6 routers and operating systems provide equivalent firewalls without relying on address translation:

  • Home routers ship with stateful firewalls that block unsolicited inbound IPv6 traffic by default.
  • Operating systems include host firewalls that apply to both IPv4 and IPv6. Windows Defender Firewall, for instance, treats both families identically.
  • For advanced users, IPv6 still supports additional layers such as iptables/nftables or macOS’s pf.

Therefore, losing NAT does not expose you to new attacks as long as your firewall remains active. What changes is transparency: you must now explicitly manage firewall policies rather than relying on NAT side-effects.

Geolocation Differences

IP geolocation databases matured on IPv4. For IPv6, the data is newer and sometimes less granular:

  • Some ISPs launch IPv6 with centralised routing, meaning your IPv6 traffic appears to originate from a regional hub rather than your city. This can improve privacy by obscuring your locale.
  • Other ISPs embed geographic hints in the delegated prefix, aiding accuracy similar to IPv4.
  • Because IPv6 addresses are rarely shared, a static address may pinpoint a single household if combined with other signals. Privacy extensions mitigate this by rotating the visible portion.

As IPv6 adoption rises, expect geolocation accuracy to improve. Stay mindful that opting out of IPv6 entirely simply because of geolocation concerns can be counter-productive; better to ensure privacy extensions are working.

VPNs and IPv6: Avoiding Leaks

VPNs historically prioritised IPv4. If your device has native IPv6 connectivity and your VPN fails to tunnel or block it, you risk IPv6 traffic bypassing the VPN entirely. To stay safe:

  1. Choose VPNs with full dual-stack support. Premium providers advertise IPv6 leak protection, either by tunnelling IPv6 traffic or by disabling it at the virtual adapter level.
  2. Test regularly. Visit ipleak.net or browserleaks.com/ip while connected. You should see only the VPN’s IPv4 and (if offered) IPv6 addresses.
  3. Disable IPv6 as a fallback. If your VPN lacks support, temporarily disable IPv6 on the host or router. This is a blunt instrument but preferable to silent leaks.

Remember to re-enable IPv6 once you upgrade to a leak-safe provider; native IPv6 offers performance improvements and compatibility with future services.

Real-World Scenarios and Guidance

Home user with ISP-supplied router

Most UK ISPs now enable IPv6 by default. Check your router’s admin page for “IPv6” status. Ensure the firewall is on, privacy extensions are enabled on your devices (usually automatic), and your VPN—if you use one—supports IPv6. No further changes are required.

Remote worker on corporate VPN

Some corporate VPNs disable IPv6 to simplify access control. Confirm your employer’s policy. If IPv6 is disabled, your personal browsing outside the VPN may still use IPv6; compartmentalise work and personal traffic via separate profiles or devices.

Self-hosted services

If you run a server at home, IPv6 removes the need for port forwarding. However, be aware that your service is reachable globally. Harden with authentication, TLS, and firewall rules limiting which IPs may connect. Keep static addresses for services, but continue using privacy extensions for client applications.

Configuration Checklist

  • Verify Privacy Extensions: On Windows, run Get-NetIPInterface in PowerShell and ensure TemporaryAddresses is Enabled. On macOS/iOS, they are always on. On Linux, check /proc/sys/net/ipv6/conf/all/use_tempaddr equals 2.
  • Inspect delegated prefix: Your router UI or ip -6 addr command shows the prefix length. A /56 or /64 is typical for residential service.
  • Run leak tests: With and without VPNs active, use online tools to confirm external visibility matches expectations.
  • Audit firewall rules: Ensure your router and OS firewalls have explicit IPv6 rules mirroring IPv4 protections. Add exceptions deliberately rather than relying on defaults.

FAQ

Should I disable IPv6 for privacy?

Usually not. Disabling IPv6 can break modern services and undermines future-proofing. It is better to ensure privacy extensions and firewalls are working, and to use VPNs that support IPv6.

Can websites fingerprint me via IPv6?

They can if your IPv6 address is static and you lack privacy extensions. With temporary addresses, the fingerprinting surface is similar to IPv4 and still dominated by browser-level signals.

Does IPv6 make me more hackable?

Not if your firewall is on. Attackers can theoretically scan IPv6 addresses, but the address space is vast, and modern operating systems treat unsolicited inbound traffic cautiously. Security hygiene—updates, strong passwords, multi-factor authentication—remains the decisive factor.

What about IPv6-only services?

Major platforms increasingly offer IPv6-only endpoints with NAT64/DNS64 translation for legacy clients. Enabling IPv6 ensures you can reach these services without latency penalties or translation quirks.

Key Takeaway

IPv6 does not inherently erode privacy. In many scenarios it strengthens it by reducing reliance on bottlenecked NATs and by enabling modern security protocols. The caveat is configuration: keep privacy extensions enabled, verify your VPN tunnels IPv6, and treat firewall rules as first-class citizens for both IP families. With those safeguards in place, you can embrace IPv6’s speed and resilience without surrendering the protections you rely on today.
