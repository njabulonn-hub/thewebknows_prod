Google’s Topics API: How It Works and What It Means

11 November 2025 13 min read

Overview: Google’s Privacy Sandbox is a sweeping attempt to reinvent digital advertising for the post-cookie era. Its flagship component, the Topics API, shifts targeting from individual-level tracking to on-device interest curation. Instead of letting third parties follow you across sites with unique identifiers, Chrome now summarises your recent browsing into a handful of high-level categories and shares them with participating sites. This explainer demystifies Topics, surveys the other Sandbox APIs, and lays out how users can keep control as the ecosystem evolves.

Overview (TL;DR)

The Topics API assigns up to five interest categories per week based on the sites you visit. When a site requests your topics, Chrome randomly returns one category from each of the past three weeks (so up to three topics total). No raw browsing history, identifiers, or sensitive categories are exposed. Topics lives alongside Protected Audiences (remarketing) and Attribution Reporting (measurement). It is more private than third-party cookies but still an advertising feature. Users can inspect or disable it in Chrome’s settings; privacy-focused browsers such as Firefox and Brave decline to implement it.

Privacy Sandbox at a Glance

The Sandbox bundles several APIs, each replacing a function previously handled by trackers:

  • Topics API: Supplies broad interests for contextual targeting.
  • Protected Audiences API (FLEDGE): Enables remarketing by running on-device auctions between ad buyers, shielding user membership lists from advertisers.
  • Attribution Reporting API: Measures ad conversions using aggregated reports and event-level pings without per-user tracking.
  • Private Aggregation, Shared Storage, Fenced Frames: Additional building blocks for anonymised insights and sandboxed creative rendering.

Collectively, these APIs aim to satisfy advertisers’ requirements while reducing cross-site surveillance.

How Topics Are Generated

Chrome maps each site you visit to one or more topics drawn from a publicly documented taxonomy (roughly 470 entries). The mapping can occur via Google’s classifier or a site-provided header indicating its desired topic. Each week, the browser stores your five most frequent topics. The rolling three-week window means a maximum of 15 topics live on your device at any time. Topics linked to sensitive categories are excluded, and lower-confidence classifications may be discarded entirely.

What Sites Learn from Topics

When a page calls the Topics API, the browser:

  1. Checks that the caller or its advertising partners have recently observed you (to prevent abuse).
  2. Randomly picks one topic from each of the last three weeks (or fewer if you have not accumulated five topics in a given week).
  3. Returns those topics to the site, which may share them with its demand partners or use them for contextual ad selection.

The site now knows that your recent browsing included, for example, “Cycling & Fitness”, “Travel & Tourism”, and “Jazz & Blues”. It does not learn which specific pages led to those topics, nor does it receive a persistent identifier. Because the selection is random and shifts every week, the signal is intentionally fuzzy.

Comparing Topics to Third-Party Cookies

Third-party cookies enabled granular, deterministic tracking across a vast network of publishers. Topics rewrites this model:

  • Scope: Cookies reveal exact URLs and timestamps; Topics shares only broad categories.
  • Persistence: Cookies can last for years; Topics data expires after three weeks.
  • User visibility: Cookies hide in the browser; Topics surfaces controls in Chrome’s UI and documents the taxonomy.
  • Cross-browser behaviour: Topics currently exists only in Chrome-based browsers; other engines reject it, limiting cross-browser profiling.

Advertisers lose granularity, particularly for niche interests and high-frequency remarketing. Users gain transparency and a default privacy baseline, but ads remain interest-driven.

Protected Audiences and Attribution Reporting

Topics is only one pillar. The Protected Audiences API allows sites to add users to remarketing audiences (“interested in discontinued trainers”). When you later visit a publisher, Chrome runs a local auction between interest groups to decide which ad shows, without exposing the membership lists. Attribution Reporting replaces pixel-based conversion tracking with aggregated reports and limited event-level pings, inhibiting user-level linking.

These complementary APIs ensure advertisers still have measurement and performance levers even without third-party cookies.

User Controls and Enterprise Policies

Chrome 115+ provides granular controls under Settings → Privacy and security → Ad privacy. You can inspect assigned topics, block individual categories, disable the Topics API entirely, and manage Protected Audiences and Measurement separately. Enterprise administrators can disable the entire Privacy Sandbox or specific APIs via group policies—useful for regulated industries or privacy-focused organisations.

Privacy Considerations and Critiques

Privacy advocates raise several concerns:

  • Residual fingerprinting: Although coarse, the combination of topics could aid fingerprinting when merged with other signals.
  • Classifier bias: The taxonomy and site-to-topic mappings are curated by Google, raising questions about accuracy and representation.
  • Competition: Publishers worry that Google’s control over both browser and ad stack may entrench its dominance.
  • Opt-out friction: Users must disable the feature manually; many will never discover the setting.

Regulators, particularly in the EU and UK, are scrutinising the Sandbox under competition law, requiring Google to adjust rollout plans and governance.

What Users Can Do

If you prefer to minimise advertising data sharing:

  1. Disable Ad privacy features: In Chrome, toggle off Topics, Protected Audiences, and Measurement. Repeat on every profile.
  2. Use alternative browsers: Firefox, Brave, Safari, and others do not implement Topics, sticking with existing privacy controls.
  3. Combine with tracker blocking: Extensions like uBlock Origin continue to block behavioural scripts regardless of Topics status.
  4. Monitor updates: The Sandbox APIs evolve rapidly. Revisit settings after major Chrome releases to ensure preferences persist.

FAQ

Can Topics be used to infer sensitive interests?

Google excludes sensitive categories (health, religion, sexuality) from the taxonomy and removes topics that appear infrequently to limit re-identification risk. However, oversight relies on Google’s curation and developer compliance.

Will advertisers still be able to remarket to me?

Yes, via the Protected Audiences API. Remarketing auctions run locally, so advertisers can target prior visitors without learning who they are.

Does Topics work in Incognito mode?

No. Incognito disables topics collection and resets existing data at window close, keeping sessions isolated.

Is Topics enabled on mobile?

Chrome on Android includes Topics with the same controls. iOS uses WebKit and currently does not expose Topics.

Key Takeaway

The Topics API represents a compromise: less invasive than third-party cookies yet still geared toward ad personalisation. Treat it as a signpost that the advertising industry is shifting to on-device computation and aggregated signals. If you value relevance and are comfortable with coarse interest sharing, the default may suffice. If privacy is paramount, disable the Sandbox features or choose a browser that forgoes them entirely.
